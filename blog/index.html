The Null Set

You don't know what you don't know. When it comes to finding solutions in software engineering, the analogy is this: not only do you need to find a needle in a haystack, but you do not know which haystack to begin searching in a multitude of haystacks. And there are similar looking needles that are not the exact needle you are looking for. And sometimes the needle may have have been in one location but since relocated. I wish I were joking.

Struggling is inevitable, inescapable. The key to success is to approach the situation in a positive light. Embrace the challenge. To exercise your capability in the face of a worthy opponent should be viewed as a wonderful opportunity to be rewarded with triumph when you overcome the adversity. Identify the calming peace that a solution exists so your effort is not futile. Remember, the answer is out there, you just have to find it. Nothing separates you from an esteemed software engineer other than the fact that they have polished their insight regarding how to discover the resolution.

Understand that an experienced software engineer (SWE) does not live in constant turmoil of struggling nor is immune to getting stuck on an issue. No, s/he has developed and nurtured the balanced habit of effectively and efficiently progressing through a problem when faced with one. This habit includes pursuing a solution in a reasonable amount of time to focus on a problem versus giving up too quickly. An experienced SWE has amassed the crucial, trusted documentation to research and its respective places on the Internet. A seasoned engineer identifies when he or she is experiencing burn out and distances themselves from the problem for the day, ready to return to the issue reenergized after a good night's rest (afternoon nap?). And finally, realizing that the scope of the problem is better handled by your superior: maybe the biggest element surrounding getting stuck had more to do with your pride rather than your intelligence?

Identify who you are as a software engineer and strive to behave that way every day. Are you comfortable accepting that you do not know all aspects of the project you are working on? Are you willing to be disciplined and commit to hours of researching when you need to inform yourself of supplementary knowledge surrounding your specific issue? Are you patient with yourself when you feel like giving up? Do you have the awareness to ask for help when you realize that you have tried your best? Most experienced software engineers demonstrate the mastery of their emotional quotient as opposed to their I.Q. Once you find closure that we all possess an empty set of optimal tactics to utilize regarding struggling at the beginning of our career, it is easier to begin filling our empty set of behavioral tools with habits to thrive in our field.

I was planning on recreating that scene from Shrek, except replace the words Ogres with "dark web" while refining the grammar. Aside from realizing that I wasn't even being consistent, that movie is a comedy classic and I would only be soiling its comedic integrity. The media sensationalizes the "dark web." Most notably, the "dark web" is associated with the Silk Road, an online drug marketplace, secret double agents, and technologically sophisticated pedophiles. I'm not naive enough to state that this doesn't exist on the "dark web," but these types of sites have the same chance of existing on the normal web. But if the "dark web"'s reputation is notorious and stained with negative publicity, how does the normal Chinese citizen or whistleblower stay anonymous to protect their identity and browse the web without inadvertently stumbling into the nefarious parts of the web or incriminate themselves? That's where I come in.

What I find most thrilling is my support in informing and educating one in the Tor (the onion routing) network. Ah, feels so much better to evade its misnomer "dark web." Individuals traverse through the connected Tor network on the app called the Tor Browser. When first installed, the app comes built in with a list of location and public keys for each directory authority. The directory authorities are trusted servers that maintain the list of 6,000+ relays of volunteers. As you enter information into your browser, the client first connects to the directory authorities. It coordinates the location of 3 updated relays and generates a key-pair with all three nodes. This established random 3 hop path is known as a circuit before reaching the destination website. Before it sends the data out into the Tor network, the data is encrypted in three layers; each layer includes the IP address of the next node. The Guard (first relay) has been identified by the directory authority as a trusted entrance to begin the process. As the data is obtained, the node decrypts the outermost layer of your encrypted data before transferring it to the next IP address listed. The Middle relay does the same. Finally, it arrives at the Exit Relay. This final relay decrypts the last layer of encryption until identifying the destination website along with the unencrypted data and passes it along. I'll just stop here before it gets way more descriptive, but that is the gist of understanding what the Tor network truly is. Congratulations! If you understood that, then you know more than the average individual who still refers to this complex system as the "dark web."

The Tor Project is the team of individuals who helped shape the Tor network and the medium to browse it: the Tor Browser. Anyone who wishes to protect their privacy and access the web freely use Tor. Tor helps protect journalists, human rights defenders, domestic violence victims, academic researchers, and anyone experiencing censorship or surveillance. And in order to help secure those individuals, a diverse set of users, developers, relay operators, and advocates from around the world volunteer to make up the participants of the community. I am proud to serve at the Frontdesk, helping individuals better understand what Tor is and how to utilize the Tor Browser. I was fortunate enough to be given the opportunity to learn about Tor...to the point where I am now an ambassador of online freedom by volunteering to run a bridge relay in my own home :)
